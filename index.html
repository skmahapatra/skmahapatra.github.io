PhD Research

My research explores the dynamic field of continuum robotics, inspired by natureâ€™s flexible structures like elephant trunks and octopus arms. Unlike traditional rigid robots, continuum robots feature a compliant, flexible backbone that allows them to maneuver through cluttered, constrained spaces. This unique flexibility makes them well-suited for delicate tasks, safe human-robot interaction, and applications ranging from minimally invasive surgery to industrial inspection.

I focus specifically on Cable-Driven Continuum Robots (CCRs), where a set of actuated cables controls the robotâ€™s shape and motion. My work tackles key challenges in this domain â€” from developing accurate kinematic and static models for diverse CCR configurations to addressing the complexities of inverse kinematics and shape control. I also explore practical applications, including compliant robotic grippers and machine learning-based methods for enhanced control and precision. The following sections detail these key research thrusts.

In this research on the kinematics of Cable-Driven Continuum Robots (CCRs), I focus on modeling and understanding the complex relationship between cable actuation and the resulting robot shape and end-effector pose. Unlike rigid robots, CCRs exhibit continuous deformation, making their kinematics inherently nonlinear and challenging. I have developed analytical and geometry-based optimization models to capture the behavior of various CCR configurations, accounting for factors such as cable routing and backbone compliance. These models not only enable precise forward and inverse kinematics solutions but also facilitate shape control, which is critical for applications requiring adaptability in constrained environments.

This research extends the kinematics and statics of Cable-Driven Continuum Robots (CCRs) to environments with obstacles. The forward kinematics model was augmented with inequality constraints to represent obstacle boundaries, allowing prediction of contact points via Lagrange multipliers. A virtual work-based method was developed to estimate contact forces. The approach was experimentally validated using 3D-printed CCRs and load cell measurements, enabling accurate shape and force prediction during interaction.

This research addresses two key inverse problems in Cable-Driven Continuum Robots (CCRs). The first involves determining a general cable routing to achieve a desired backbone shape, solved using the discretized four-bar model. The second focuses on identifying the pre-bent backbone shape that deforms into the target shape under actuation, using a strain energy minimization approach. This method also enables designing CCRs to operate effectively around obstacles.

This research explores the application of continuum robotics principles to the design of novel, compliant grippers. The developed inverse modeling techniques enabled the design of continuum-based grippers. A three-fingered gripper was created to conform to an objectâ€™s contour, ensuring stable grasping with multiple contact points. Additionally, a cable-routed gripper was demonstrated to grasp and reorient a cube in 3D, showcasing precise shape control for manipulation tasks.

To overcome the computational complexity of traditional inverse kinematic models, this research investigates the use of machine learning and neural networks. We explored the use of feed-forward neural networks (FNNs) to solve the inverse kinematics of Cable-Driven Continuum Robots (CCRs). For CCRs with straight-routed cables, FNNs were trained effectively due to the presence of unique solutions. In cases with generally routed cables, a reconstruction-based loss function was introduced to address the problem of multiple inverse solutions. A forward kinematics model was first trained and integrated into the inverse model's training. The networks demonstrated high predictive accuracy, showcasing the promise of machine learning for CCR kinematics.

Master's Thesis

This work focuses on a classic control systems problem: the Twin Rotor Multiple-Input Multiple-Output (MIMO) system, which serves as a laboratory model for helicopter flight dynamics. The research involves deriving a comprehensive dynamic model using bond graph techniques and developing advanced control strategies. The goal is to achieve stable and precise control over the helicopter's pitch and yaw angles through co-simulation with Adams and MATLAB as well as bondgraph separately, addressing the system's cross-coupling and nonlinearities.
